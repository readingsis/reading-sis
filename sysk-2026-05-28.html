Howard Hughes in Las Vegas

Hosts of Stuff You Should Know
28 May 2026 28 min read 1h 15m
TL;DR

Howard Hughes, a billionaire aviation and film mogul, retreated to the Desert Inn hotel in Las Vegas in 1966 and never left—eventually buying the entire property. His extreme germaphobia, opioid addiction, and untreated mental illness combined with unlimited wealth created a surreal situation where he controlled entire aspects of the city from a darkened hotel room, including buying a TV station to rerun his favorite movies and ordering 200 gallons of ice cream at a time.

About the show
›
Stuff You Should Know is a podcast from iHeart Radio where hosts Josh Clark and Chuck Bryant explore the fascinating histories and surprising details behind everyday topics. In this episode, they dive deep into Howard Hughes' eccentric and increasingly isolated life in Las Vegas, examining how one of America's most powerful and wealthy industrialists became a recluse on the ninth floor of a hotel he eventually bought.
Takeaways
1
Wealth without accountability amplifies mental illness Hughes' combination of vast fortune, unlimited ability to have demands met without question, and declining mental health created a perfect storm where his behavior became increasingly extreme and unchecked. His employees couldn't refuse him, his family had no control, and he faced no social or professional consequences for his actions, allowing his OCD and germaphobia to spiral into complete isolation.
2
Germaphobia and self-neglect created a paradoxical lifestyle Hughes' obsessive fear of others' germs was paired with complete neglect of his own hygiene—he bathed once yearly, grew long fingernails, urinated in bottles, and wore tissue boxes as shoes. This suggests his condition was not standard germaphobia but a complex psychological disorder where he distinguished between 'contamination' from others versus his own bodily waste.
3
One man's impulse purchases reshaped an entire city Hughes' casual decision to buy six hotel-casinos between 1966-1968 to avoid eviction fundamentally changed Las Vegas from a mob-run town to a legitimate business center. His purchases, made through intermediaries without him ever appearing publicly, inadvertently triggered the Nevada Corporate Gaming Act and modernized the city's gaming industry.
4
Isolation enabled by infrastructure and staff loyalty Hughes maintained his complete retreat from reality through a carefully constructed system: his right-hand man Robert Mayhew handled all external affairs without ever meeting him in person, and the 'Mormon Mafia'—six loyalists hired specifically for their non-drinking, non-gambling values—served as his only physical contact with the outside world.
5
Selective indulgence revealed priorities amid decline Rather than basic self-care, Hughes' fixations focused on entertainment (buying a TV station to rerun movies) and specific foods (ordering 200 gallons of ice cream). His documented consumption of Ice Station Zebra 150 times and detailed instructions for opening canned fruit reveal obsessive patterns that eclipsed nutrition or hygiene.
6
Opioid addiction from injury created decades of decline Hughes' initial prescription for pain following a plane crash led to a morphine and opioid addiction by the 1950s that persisted for decades. This addiction, combined with his pre-existing mental health issues, created a cascade of deterioration that transformed him from a public figure into a recluse by 1961, well before his Vegas isolation.
