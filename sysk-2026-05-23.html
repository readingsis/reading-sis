TL;DR

Flight attendants undergo grueling 7–12 week training where 95% of time focuses on safety equipment and emergency response, not customer service. Despite the "sexy stewardess" era of the 1960s-70s when airlines marketed them as attractions with restrictive appearance and age rules, modern flight attendants are highly trained safety professionals capable of saving lives—yet passengers often underestimate them and treat them poorly.

About the show
›
Stuff You Should Know is a deep-dive podcast exploring how things work. In this Selects rerun episode, hosts Josh Clark and Chuck Bryant examine the real job of flight attendants—covering their rigorous training, demanding schedules, and critical safety responsibilities. Guest pilot Captain Jerry Rowland adds firsthand aviation perspective.
Takeaways
1
95% of training is emergency response, not beverage service Flight attendant training consists primarily of safety equipment knowledge, written tests (90% minimum pass), and 100% practical exams on simulated emergencies—from smoke evacuation to water landings. Customer service skills receive minimal focus despite being what passengers see. Airlines structure training this way because they must ensure crew can react instinctively during crises.
2
Buddy passes create liability for flight attendants Flight attendants can share free/discounted standby passes with friends and family, but if the passenger misbehaves, the attendant faces consequences—potentially losing perks entirely. This creates a perverse incentive where attendants must vet and coach every beneficiary, explaining why many avoid the perk altogether despite its apparent value.
3
Training is 7–12 weeks, six days, 12 hours daily Called "Barbie boot camp," trainees must arrive in full work uniform, makeup, and professional appearance—simulating actual flight conditions. This grueling schedule mirrors the real workday attendants will face. Airlines use this approach to expose candidates to physical and mental demands upfront; most washouts occur during the high-stress emergency drills.
4
Airlines were explicitly sexist until very recently Through the 1950s–70s, airlines enforced strict age limits (forced retirement at 32), weight caps (120 lbs), marital status bans, and no-children rules. Advertisers marketed flight attendants as attractions—Eastern Airlines gave male passengers "little black books" to collect attendant phone numbers. These weren't federal rules but company policies reflecting industry-wide bias.
5
Ellen Church—nurse pilot—pitched the first female crew in 1930 When Boeing Air Transport (later United) rejected her idea, Church reframed it as psychology: "How can a man say he's afraid to fly if he knows a woman is piloting?" The airline hired eight women for a three-month trial. It worked so well the practice became permanent, establishing women in aviation nearly a century ago.
6
Post-9/11 security stress shapes passenger behavior A veteran flight attendant interviewed for the episode noted that before 9/11, flying was an "event" people were excited about. Now, after TSA lines, shoe removal, and laptop checks, passengers arrive exhausted and crabby—before boarding. This stress cascade means attendants face angrier clientele despite doing the same job, partly explaining why kindness matters more than ever.
7
Annual recertification and new aircraft training is mandatory Beyond initial 7–12 week training, flight attendants must return yearly for recertification on new procedures and regulatory updates. When airlines deploy new aircraft, the entire crew retrains because safety equipment placement and procedures differ. This ensures crews stay current with evolving safety standards and fleet changes.
