TL;DR

Success in fundraising and entrepreneurship hinges on three non-negotiable elements: mental toughness to overcome self-doubt, the ability to sell yourself confidently in 30–45 seconds, and understanding your unit economics before you launch. Kickstarter works best for products with clear timelines (12–18 months) and proven demand, not billion-dollar moonshots.

About Everette Taylor
›
Everette Taylor is the CEO of Kickstarter, the world's largest crowdfunding platform. He has built a career in startups, product launches, and fundraising, and now leads the company that has enabled creators to raise billions for their projects. In this episode, he shares his playbook for pitching, building trust with audiences, and understanding when crowdfunding—versus venture capital—is the right path.
Takeaways
1
**Confidence sells; self-deprecation kills deals** Everette describes 'delusional confidence' as essential—people with zero dollars but unshakeable belief in their pitch outperform hesitant pitchers with better credentials. The moment you lead with reasons why someone shouldn't invest in you (even to seem humble), you've already lost. Speaking confidently creates belief; doubt shows through instantly.
2
**Unit economics trump everything else** The single most common failure on Kickstarter is underestimating costs. Creators price products at $50 without accounting for manufacturing, shipping, tariffs, and packaging—then discover they lose money on every unit. Always include a 10–30% buffer (depending on product complexity) and use Kickstarter's pledge manager to charge shipping dynamically after campaign success.
3
**Crowdfunding and VC solve different problems** Kickstarter works for products you can deliver in 12–18 months with proven demand; VCs fund billion-dollar bets. If you're doing a consumer hardware product with real unit economics, Kickstarter is often the better path because you keep equity and maintain control. VC money comes with board pressure for 100x+ returns, which can force bad decisions (like rejecting a profitable exit).
4
**Master the 30-45 second pitch or lose them** Attention spans are shorter than ever. Everette sees most pitches fail because they bury the lede or lack conciseness. The winning formula: humanize your idea, make it relatable, and demonstrate that you understand your audience's immediate needs—not 10-year plans. This applies whether you're pitching VCs (long-term vision) or Kickstarter backers (short-term delivery).
5
**Skill-based currency opens investor doors** If you lack connections or capital, build relationships by offering a valuable skill. Everette used marketing expertise to trade for introductions to investors, bypassing the need for existing networks. Identify what you can give others (strategy advice, design, content, etc.) and use that as negotiation leverage for the connections you need.
6
**Video is the #1 predictor of Kickstarter success** Everette sees this pattern repeatedly: campaigns with compelling videos succeed; those without almost always fail. People won't read long copy anymore. A 30-second video that shows passion, builds trust, and demonstrates your ability to deliver outperforms pages of text. Mobile-first, story-driven video is now the default expectation.
7
**Build urgency and exclusivity into pre-orders** A discount (e.g., $125 instead of $200) or exclusive color/variant available only on Kickstarter creates urgency. Without it, backers ask 'Why not just wait for retail?' Combine scarcity, exclusivity, value, timeliness, and demonstrated trust (clear manufacturing plan, communication strategy, proven track record) to drive conversions and reduce risk of underperformance.
8
**Mental toughness and emotional regulation are non-negotiable** Everette identifies these as the #1 and #2 traits for entrepreneurs. Self-doubt bleeds through in your pitch, marketing, and leadership; if you're struggling with imposter syndrome or lack mental fortitude, people feel it. Therapy, reflection, and deliberate confidence-building aren't optional—they directly impact your ability to fundraise and execute.
