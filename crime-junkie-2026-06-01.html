SERIAL KILLER: David Meirhofer

Case: The Abduction of Susie Jaeger
1 Jun 2026 4 min read 45m
TL;DR

Seven-year-old Susie Jaeger was snatched from a Montana campground in 1973 by David Meirhofer, a local man who then sadistically tormented her family with calls and letters. The case became a landmark for the FBI's nascent criminal profiling unit, which helped link Meirhofer to multiple murders. Susie's mother, Marietta Jaeger, played a direct role in extracting a confession by keeping Meirhofer on the phone long enough for investigators to act.

About the Case
›
In June 1973, seven-year-old Susie Jaeger was abducted from a family campsite in Montana, vanishing without a trace and leaving investigators with almost no leads. The case took a disturbing turn when the perpetrator began making taunting phone calls and sending letters to the Jaeger family. The break came through an unlikely partnership between Susie's determined mother, Marietta Jaeger, and the FBI's newly formed behavioral profiling unit, ultimately exposing serial killer David Meirhofer.
Takeaways
1
Victim's mother directly enabled the break Marietta Jaeger refused to remain a passive bystander and actively worked with the FBI, keeping Meirhofer on the phone for an extended call that provided investigators with the evidence needed to identify him. Her involvement challenges the assumption that families are only peripheral to investigations. This case became a model for how victim families can be strategic assets rather than witnesses to be shielded.
2
FBI profiling unit cracked its earliest major case The Jaeger case is one of the founding examples of the FBI's Behavioral Science Unit — later immortalized in pop culture — doing real operational work. Profilers told investigators to look locally, at someone with community camouflage, which directly pointed toward Meirhofer. This was 1973, years before criminal profiling became formalized doctrine, making the outcome historically significant.
3
Perpetrator taunted family over multiple years Meirhofer sent letters and made phone calls to the Jaegers across an extended period, a pattern of post-crime contact that investigators now recognize as a hallmark of certain predator profiles seeking continued psychological dominance. His anniversary call was not impulsive — it was calculated. This behavior ultimately became his undoing by giving investigators direct contact with him.
4
Single confession unlocked answers for four families Meirhofer's eventual confession revealed he was responsible for the deaths of at least four victims, meaning three other families had been living without answers until this case broke. The serial nature of his crimes had gone undetected in part because the victims and locations were disparate. The Jaeger investigation's resolution had a cascading effect on multiple cold cases simultaneously.
5
Ordinary community standing shielded a serial killer Meirhofer was not a drifter or obvious outsider — he was embedded in the local Montana community, which is precisely why investigators struggled to identify him for so long. The profiling unit's insight that the killer would appear 'normal' was counterintuitive to the investigation at the time. This case reinforced the danger of assuming predators self-select out of community life.
