SERIAL KILLER: David Meirhofer

Case: The Montana Serial Killer
1 Jun 2026 9 min read 45m
TL;DR

In 1973, seven-year-old Susie Jaeger vanished from a Montana campground, and the kidnapper taunted her family with calls and letters for years. The case broke when investigators partnered with the FBI's new behavioral profiling unit and a determined mother, revealing a serial killer responsible for at least four victims and leading to the conviction of David Meirhofer.

Takeaways
1
Behavioral profiling emerged as a turning point for law enforcement The FBI's newly formed behavioral profiling unit played a critical role in cracking the Meirhofer case, representing an early success for psychological criminal analysis. This case became one of the landmark examples that validated the profiling methodology during its foundational years in the 1970s.
2
Harassment communications revealed pattern and psychology The killer's continued contact with the Jaeger family through calls and letters—rather than disappearing—became a critical investigative thread. This compulsive need to maintain connection to victims and their families is a psychological signature that helped identify and profile the perpetrator.
3
Persistent family involvement accelerated case resolution Susie Jaeger's mother's determination to pursue leads and work with investigators was instrumental in breaking through years of stalled investigation. The case demonstrates how engaged families can push law enforcement to pursue connections and evidence they might otherwise overlook.
4
Multi-victim pattern emerged from single disappearance What began as an investigation into one child's abduction eventually uncovered connections to at least three additional victims. The ability to link cases geographically and temporally revealed a serial killer who had evaded detection across multiple jurisdictions.
