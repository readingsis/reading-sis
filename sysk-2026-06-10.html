Short Stuff: Rain Barrels!

Josh Clark and Chuck Bryant
10 Jun 2026 5 min read 14m
TL;DR

Rain barrels are simple water-harvesting systems that have been used for thousands of years. While rainwater isn't drinkable, it's ideal for gardens and car washing because it's naturally soft and free of treated minerals—but your roof material, filtration, and overflow management all matter significantly.

About the show
›
Stuff You Should Know is a long-running podcast where hosts Josh Clark and Chuck Bryant explore fascinating topics in accessible, conversational depth. In this Short Stuff episode, they dive into rain barrels—how to collect and use rainwater, the math behind water harvesting, roof material considerations, and practical maintenance tips for a sustainable home system.
Takeaways
1
Roof material directly contaminates collected water Asphalt shingles leach harmful compounds, treated cedar shakes contain arsenic, and metal roofs are coated with protective PAS that all end up in your water. The cleanest options are slate, terracotta, or ceramic tiles—but these are expensive luxury roofing. Most homeowners need robust filtration if using standard roofing materials.
2
Overflow must drain away from your foundation Without a proper overflow mechanism directing excess water into a downspout, filled rain barrels spill directly down the side of your house into the foundation. This can cause expensive structural damage. An overflow pipe from the barrel rim to a downspout is essential infrastructure.
3
A 1,200 sq ft roof yields 720 gallons per inch The formula is simple: square footage of roof × 0.6 gallons per square foot per inch of rainfall = total gallons harvestable. A typical roof collects substantial water from even modest rainfall, making overflow management and proper drainage critical to avoid foundation damage.
4
Rainwater softness makes it superior for plants Rainwater is naturally soft—lacking the hard minerals found in tap water—making it ideal for gardens and indoor plants. Treated tap water contains chlorine and sanitation chemicals that harm plants, which is why collectors let water sit for days or use filters. Rainwater achieves the same purity benefit without chemicals.
5
Sealed lids prevent mosquitoes and algae growth Rain barrels must have tight-fitting lids to block sunlight (which triggers algae growth), prevent debris accumulation, keep children safe, and—critically—prevent mosquitoes from breeding. Mosquitoes lay larvae in standing water, turning an unlidded barrel into a public health hazard.
6
Freezing climates require winter winterization In cold climates, water trapped in barrels expands when frozen, cracking the barrel. Winterization means disconnecting the barrel from gutters, draining it completely, and storing it indoors during cold months.
