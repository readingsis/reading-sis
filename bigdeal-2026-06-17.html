TL;DR

Eric Jorgenson argues that Elon Musk's defining edge isn't genius or work ethic alone — it's being so mission-driven that financial risk becomes irrelevant. The clearest proof: in 2008, with only $30M left, he split it between both a dying SpaceX and a near-bankrupt Tesla rather than save one, because he couldn't bear to let either mission die. Jorgenson distills this into a replicable algorithm: find the bottleneck, attack it with total intensity right now, and delete everything that isn't essential.

About Eric Jorgenson
›
Eric Jorgenson is an author and investor best known for writing The Almanack of Naval Ravikant. His latest book, The Book of Elon, synthesizes the mental models, habits, and philosophy of Elon Musk drawn from interviews, speeches, and firsthand accounts. Naval Ravikant has called his work essential reading for entrepreneurs.
Takeaways
1
Escalate meeting cadence exactly where the bottleneck is Elon's management style ties meeting frequency directly to bottleneck severity: normal issues get weekly syncs, critical ones go to daily, and in extreme cases he's reportedly required hourly email updates around the clock for a full week. This is a concrete operational tool — not just cultural pressure but a formal ratchet mechanism that makes stalled problems impossible to ignore.
2
Attack the bottleneck with total intensity, immediately Jorgenson identifies Elon's core operating edge as always working on the single highest-leverage constraint in his business right now — not incrementally, but with a massive improvement. During Model 3 production hell, Elon moved into the factory and literally ran to wherever red lights were flashing on the line. The compounding effect of right thing × right time × massive improvement multiplies rather than adds.
3
Delete more than you think — then add 10% back Elon's engineering algorithm requires removing more than necessary until something breaks, then adding back only what's essential. His rule: if you're not restoring roughly 10% of what you deleted, you haven't deleted enough. This forces teams to discover what is truly load-bearing versus what is organizational habit or overengineering.
4
Mission conviction makes financial risk irrelevant Elon's 2008 decision to split his last $30M between two dying companies wasn't irrational — it was the logical output of caring more about the mission than the money. Jorgenson argues that most people treat wealth as the goal, but for Elon money is an incidental byproduct of product and mission obsession. This reframes how founders should think about risk tolerance: it's not about courage, it's about how much you actually care.
5
CEOs should fire their scheduler to own their priority Elon reportedly fired his scheduler not out of frustration but because he wanted full control over where his attention goes at any moment. Jorgenson frames this as 'passive delegation' — by focusing only on the highest priority, you implicitly trust the team to handle everything else. A calendar set two weeks ago cannot know where today's bottleneck is.
6
Pick problems where you'd be proud to fail Jorgenson highlights that Elon evaluates SpaceX not on expected ROI but on whether contributing to the mission — even if it fails — would have been worth it. This is a fundamentally different selection criterion for what to work on: not 'what's most likely to succeed' but 'what would I be proud to have attempted even in failure.' It's a filter that naturally pushes toward high-importance, low-competition problems.
7
Fear disappears when you look at it directly Elon's quoted philosophy is 'feel the fear and do it anyway' — but Jorgenson adds his interpretation that fear is partially an illusion that dissolves under direct examination. The practical test: if you can imagine quitting what you're doing, you haven't picked the right mission yet. The right mission makes fear feel minuscule compared to the promise of the outcome.
