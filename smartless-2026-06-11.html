TL;DR

Tom Hanks reflects on his decades-long fascination with World War II storytelling—rooted in his childhood exposure to veterans who saw it as a dividing line in their lives—and explains why he keeps returning to that era: the bad guys lost, and ordinary people were asked to do extraordinary things. He shares detailed production stories from Saving Private Ryan, including how the filmmakers created terrifyingly realistic beach sequences using amputee stunt performers and controlled explosions, which gave him a visceral sense of what actual soldiers experienced.

About SmartLess
›
SmartLess is a comedy interview podcast hosted by Jason Bateman, Sean Hayes, and Will Arnett. The three friends invite guests to discuss their careers, creative processes, and personal stories while riffing on pop culture, literature, and life. Known for sharp humor and surprisingly thoughtful conversation, the show balances irreverent banter with genuine insight.
Takeaways
1
**Realism in war films requires controlled chaos and real bodies** Saving Private Ryan's beach sequences used 500 Irish Army soldiers, amputee stunt performers, harnesses, squibs, air mortars, and multiple cameras firing simultaneously to create genuine disorientation. The filmmakers deliberately removed safety flags after initial instruction so actors would stumble blindly through explosions—blending fake (harnesses, controlled effects) with real sensory overload (cold, wet, noise, genuine panic) to approximate what soldiers actually experienced.
2
**War stories root in personal witness, not history books** Tom's lifelong fascination with WWII wasn't sparked by academic study but by overhearing everyday adults—caregivers, family friends—reference it as a fundamental dividing line in their lived experience: before the war, during, and after. This suggests that authentic storytelling about historical events comes from understanding how they shaped the people around us, not abstract timelines.
3
**Proximity to death makes everyday people heroes in hindsight** Tom describes a paratrooper-turned-dry-cleaner who jumped into Normandy on D-Day and, 40 years later, still returned each June to visit the graves of friends who didn't return. The casual revelation—a man delivering pressed shirts who had faced genuine existential horror—made Tom 'feel stupid and small,' illuminating how an entire generation of 'ordinary' people carried extraordinary trauma invisibly.
4
**Ordinary people doing extraordinary things under compulsion** Tom's reverence for WWII isn't about glorifying war but about the specific moral clarity of that moment: normal schoolteachers, dry cleaners, and young men were conscripted to defeat genuinely evil regimes. His character in Saving Private Ryan embodied this—not a born soldier but someone called upon when the entire world needed liberation, which is why the film resonates decades later.
5
**Sitcom schedules are secretly the best gig in entertainment** Tom and Sean discuss sitcom work—only 3–4 days per week, 12 working days per month, 6 hours daily, with built-in breaks, catering, and craft services—as an unexpectedly lucrative and creatively fun arrangement compared to film work. A show like Bosom Buddies paid Tom nearly $10,000 in two weeks, dramatically exceeding what he made annually as a Shakespearean actor.
