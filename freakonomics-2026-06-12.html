677. Can Backgammon Save Us from Ourselves?

with Frank Frigo, Masayuki Mochizuki, Mark Olsen, Bob Wachtel
12 Jun 2026 5 min read 36m
TL;DR

Backgammon is far more than a dice game — its probabilistic decision framework has been applied to NFL fourth-down strategy, helping the Eagles and Chiefs win Super Bowls. The game's doubling cube forces players to constantly compare guaranteed small losses against risky large ones, a skill that transfers directly to business and investing. A global renaissance in in-person social gaming is driving backgammon's fastest growth in decades.

About the show
›
Freakonomics Radio is hosted by Steven Dubner and explores the hidden side of everything through an economic lens. This episode continues the show's occasional series on games, focusing on backgammon's ancient history, its gambling culture, and its surprising applications in NFL analytics and decision science. Guests include two-time world backgammon champion Frank Frigo, world #1 Masayuki 'Mochi' Mochizuki, Backgammon Galaxy co-founder Mark Olsen, and philosopher-gambler Bob Wachtel.
Takeaways
1
Backgammon theory reshaped NFL fourth-down decisions Frank Frigo's analytics firm built simulation models grounded in backgammon's probability framework and licensed them to ~12 NFL teams. The core insight — optimize for win probability, not yards or points — directly countered coaches' ingrained risk aversion. The Eagles and Chiefs used these models during their Super Bowl runs.
2
The doubling cube is a decision-science instrument Invented in the 1920s, the doubling cube forces players to explicitly compare a certain small loss (forfeit one point) against a probabilistic distribution of larger outcomes. This is structurally identical to real-world choices in investing, negotiation, and deal-making. Mark Olsen calls it the only unilaterally controllable element in a game otherwise governed by randomness.
3
Probabilistic thinking beats deterministic thinking for real-life decisions Frigo contrasts chess — where the best move often splits outcomes into 100% win or 100% loss paths — with backgammon, where every decision is probabilistic. He argues backgammon more accurately mirrors business and social decisions, which are rarely binary. This makes it a better training ground for executives and investors than chess.
4
Sunk-cost discipline is the defining trait of top players Both Frigo and Olsen independently identify ignoring sunk costs as essential to high-level backgammon. Frigo credits his 2023 world championship partly to his ability to 'turn the page' after bad luck and refocus on the current position. Olsen notes any professional games player or investor operates the same way.
5
Backgammon Galaxy reached 220K users by pivoting from gambling to social gaming Mark Olsen and co-founders initially planned a money-gaming site but found the regulatory environment too restrictive. They pivoted to subscriptions and virtual coins after their YouTube channel became the world's largest backgammon channel. The lesson: the social and educational layer of a skill game can be a larger addressable market than the gambling layer.
6
Skill gap in backgammon exceeds that of poker Frigo argues a complete novice can beat Phil Ivey roughly a third of the time at poker by simply going all-in every hand — but in a 19–25 point backgammon match, a novice has near-zero chance against a top player. The sheer volume of active decisions prevents any simplifying heuristic from closing the gap.
7
Skill-based gambling builds decision-making resilience; pure chance does not Olsen draws a sharp distinction between roulette (no informational advantage, pure vice) and backgammon, poker, or prediction markets (skill rewarded, hard work compounds). He argues that instant financial feedback on bad decisions hardens mental resilience in a way few other activities can. This reframes gambling as a training environment rather than a moral hazard.
