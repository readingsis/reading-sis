About Graham Hancock
›
Graham Hancock is an author and researcher who has spent over 30 years investigating evidence of a lost civilization predating recorded history. He argues that anatomically modern humans existed 315,000 years ago, yet organized civilization didn't emerge until 6,000 years ago—a gap he believes represents a forgotten chapter. Hancock explores ancient maps, myths, and archaeological anomalies to propose that a sophisticated pre-ice age civilization may have existed around 20,000 years ago before being wiped out by a cataclysmic comet impact 12,800 years ago.
Takeaways
1
Ancient myths encode astronomical precision Hancock references 'Hamlet's Mill,' a 1960s academic study by MIT historians showing that ancient myths worldwide contain encoded imagery and numbers related to precession of the equinoxes—an astronomical phenomenon requiring hundreds of years of precise observation. This suggests ancient civilizations possessed sophisticated astronomical knowledge typically attributed only to modern science.
2
Maps show pre-ice age coastlines and Antarctica The Piri Reis map from 1531 depicts Antarctica with accurate relative longitudes, despite Antarctica not being 'discovered' until 1820. Since accurate longitude calculation wasn't solved until the 18th century, Hancock argues this indicates an earlier civilization mapped the world during the ice age with maritime technology our civilization didn't achieve until recently.
3
Hunter-gatherers built monumental architecture Göbekli Tepe (11,600 years old) and Caral-Supe pyramids (5,500 years old) were built by pre-agricultural societies organizing large labor forces, contradicting the assumption that civilization requires agriculture. This challenges the conventional timeline and suggests missing evidence of sophisticated pre-agricultural civilizations that may have been erased by the younger Dryas impact.
