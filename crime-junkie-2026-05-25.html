MYSTERIOUS DEATH OF: The Jamison Family

Case: The Jamison Family Disappearance
25 May 2026 4 min read 33m
TL;DR

The Jamison family vanished on a remote Oklahoma mountain in 2009 under circumstances that defy every tidy explanation — murder-suicide, drug deal gone wrong, and foul play all have fatal holes. Their remains were found four years later in terrain so rugged that investigators say the family almost certainly could not have hiked there on their own, pointing strongly to foul play. With no cause of death established, no gun found, and the case no longer actively worked, whoever may be responsible appears to be getting away with it.

About the Case
›
In October 2009, Bobby and Sherilyn Jamison and their 6-year-old daughter Madison vanished from Panola Mountain in Latimer County, Oklahoma, leaving behind their locked truck, a dog near death, $32,000 in cash, and a scathing letter from Sherilyn to Bobby. The family had a history of addiction, mental illness, a bitter lawsuit against Bobby's own father, and unsettling beliefs about demons and spirits in their home. Four years later, in November 2013, a hunter found three skulls — including a child's — just 2.6 miles from the abandoned truck, but cause of death was never determined.
Takeaways
1
Remains location physically impossible to reach alone The skulls were found 2.6 miles from the truck, but in terrain so rugged that an able-bodied person in great shape would need half a day to hike it. Bobby had chronic back pain and they had a 6-year-old. Investigators and locals say it is nearly inconceivable the family got there under their own power, making foul play — likely involving transport of the bodies — the most credible explanation.
2
Family was suing Bobby's own father months before vanishing Bobby filed a lawsuit against his father Bob Jamison in 2008 over an unpaid ownership stake in a gas station. The conflict escalated to Bobby seeking a protective order, claiming his father hit him with a car in November 2008 and attempted to kill him again in April 2009 — just five months before the disappearance. This unresolved, violent family conflict makes Bob Jamison a person of significant interest that the podcast says remains underexplored.
3
$32,000 cash left behind suggests targeted foul play When the truck was found, $32,000 in cash was sitting under the seat untouched. Sheriff Beecham's theory is that a perpetrator may have taken a separate bag of similar value but simply didn't know about the hidden cash — meaning robbery was likely a motive but the killer wasn't thorough. This detail is one of the strongest arguments against a stranger-crime scenario where you'd expect everything of value to be taken.
4
Missing brown bag is the case's most critical unresolved clue Surveillance footage shows Sherilyn carrying a brown briefcase-style bag to the truck before they left. That bag was never found at the truck, the property, or the remains site. It potentially contained money, Sherilyn's missing .22 caliber gun, or both — and its disappearance is the one physical detail that most strongly suggests someone else was present and took it.
5
Original surveillance video destroyed in a flood, case went cold The full multi-angle surveillance footage from the morning of the disappearance no longer exists — the department where it was stored suffered a flood. Only short media clips survive. Combined with the fact that the deaths remain officially 'undetermined' and are no longer actively investigated, the evidentiary record has been permanently degraded, making resolution increasingly unlikely.
6
Murder-suicide theory collapses without cause of death Despite Sherilyn's documented mental instability, recent suicide attempt, and the hostile letter found in the truck, the theory falls apart because no gun was ever recovered, no cause of death was established, and the remains' location is inconsistent with a family walking to a remote spot and dying there. The hole found near a skull was officially attributed to animal activity, not a gunshot.
7
Dog locked in truck undermines murder-suicide and abandonment theories Madison was said to love the family dog, which was found emaciated and nearly dead, locked inside the truck for days. If the family had walked into the wilderness voluntarily to end their lives or start over, it's deeply inconsistent that they would leave the dog to die slowly in a locked vehicle rather than release it or bring it along. This detail is one Ashley flags as a significant logical hole in every voluntary-departure theory.
