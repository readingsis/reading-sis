TL;DR

Smiling is controlled by specific facial muscles (primarily the zygomaticus major), appears to be innate across cultures and even in congenitally blind individuals, but its meaning and acceptability have changed dramatically throughout Western history—from being associated with insanity or drunkenness in medieval Europe to becoming a cultural norm only after 1930s advertising campaigns convinced people to smile in photographs.

About the show
›
Stuff You Should Know is an iHeart Radio podcast hosted by Josh Clark and Chuck Bryant that explores fascinating topics ranging from science and history to culture and human behavior. In this episode, the hosts dive deep into the anatomy, evolution, psychology, and cultural history of smiling—examining why humans smile, how it differs across cultures, and what our facial expressions reveal about our emotions. The episode combines scientific research, historical context, and the hosts' characteristic humor and curiosity.
Takeaways
1
Different cultures interpret smiling intelligence differently A 2015 cross-cultural study of 44 countries found that China, Germany, and Egypt associate smiling with intelligence, while Japan, France, and Iran correlate it with lower intelligence. This demonstrates that despite universal facial anatomy, cultural contexts assign contradictory meanings to identical expressions, making smiles a high-risk communication tool across borders.
2
The eyes reveal genuine happiness, not deception True enjoyment smiles activate Action Unit 6 (orbicularis oculi), causing the eye cover fold to lower and eyebrows to dip slightly—movements that are difficult to consciously control. While some people can fake eye involvement, this muscle combination remains the most reliable marker of authentic emotion, though no expression is impossible to fake with practice.
3
All genuine smiles use the same core muscle Every true smile involves contraction of the zygomaticus major muscle (Action Unit 12), which pulls the corners of the mouth upward and outward. This anatomical fact is universal across all humans and explains why smiling is such a consistent social signal. Understanding this baseline helps distinguish genuine from fake expressions.
4
Smiling is innate, not learned through imitation Congenitally blind infants smile at the sound of voices at the same developmental stage as sighted babies, and blind athletes from 23 different cultures display identical spontaneous expressions to their sighted counterparts. This evidence proves smiling is hardwired human behavior, not something we learn by watching others' faces.
5
Babies strategically time smiles to maximize parental response Research shows infants as young as 4-12 weeks learn to flash smiles precisely when they'll generate the longest smiling response from caregivers—essentially manipulating adults into engagement through facial expression timing. This demonstrates that smiling serves early social bonding and may have evolved as a manipulation tool for survival.
6
Smiling was considered a sign of madness for centuries In medieval and Renaissance Europe, smiling in paintings was used exclusively to depict insanity, drunkenness, or immaturity. This cultural norm persisted for hundreds of years and carried into early photography—people didn't begin smiling in photographs until 1930s advertising campaigns by camera manufacturers actively encouraged the behavior, fundamentally reshaping social norms around facial expression.
7
Immigration-heavy nations smile more as social lubricant Countries with high immigration rates like the United States and Brazil show higher baseline smiling behavior, theorized because diverse populations use smiling as a non-verbal 'I'm friendly' signal to bridge cultural barriers. This suggests smiling frequency reflects social need rather than innate emotional state.
8
Humans evolved fewer fast-twitch facial muscles than other primates While primates rely on fast-twitch muscles for facial expression and lack speech, humans repurposed some of those fibers into slower-twitch muscles needed for speech. As a result, humans are paradoxically less facially expressive than our primate relatives despite having language—a trade-off between verbal and non-verbal communication.
